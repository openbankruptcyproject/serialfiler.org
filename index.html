Serial Bankruptcy Filing

A plain-language guide to the limits, consequences, and legal framework around filing bankruptcy more than once -- including discharge bars, automatic stay restrictions, and the 180-day filing bar under Section 109(g).

What Is Serial Filing?

Serial bankruptcy filing means filing for bankruptcy protection more than once. Sometimes this is entirely legitimate -- a debtor who completed a Chapter 7 case years ago may face a new financial crisis from job loss, divorce, or medical bills and need to file again. The Bankruptcy Code allows repeat filings, but with significant restrictions designed to prevent abuse.

In other cases, serial filing is a symptom of a deeper problem. A debtor may have been poorly advised the first time -- steered into a Chapter 13 plan they could never afford, only to have the case dismissed and then file again. Some attorneys file bare-bones petitions to trigger the automatic stay, buy time, and collect fees -- knowing the case will eventually be dismissed. When that happens, the debtor's filing history grows longer while their financial situation stays the same or gets worse.

Courts have built-in protections to address both scenarios. The key mechanisms are discharge waiting periods, the 180-day filing bar, and automatic stay limitations for repeat filers.

Discharge Bars for Repeat Filers

The Bankruptcy Code imposes waiting periods between discharge-eligible filings. If you file too soon after a prior case, you can still file -- but you will not receive a discharge of your debts. The waiting periods depend on the chapter of the prior case and the chapter of the new case:

New Case Prior Case Wait Period Statute
Chapter 7 Chapter 7 8 years Section 727(a)(8)
Chapter 7 Chapter 13 6 years* Section 727(a)(9)
Chapter 13 Chapter 7 4 years Section 1328(f)(1)
Chapter 13 Chapter 13 2 years Section 1328(f)(2)

*70% Rule Exception: The 6-year bar under Section 727(a)(9) does not apply if the debtor paid at least 70% of allowed unsecured claims in the prior Chapter 13 case and the plan was proposed in good faith with best-effort payments. This exception rewards debtors who made a genuine effort in their prior case.

All waiting periods are measured from the filing date of the prior case to the filing date of the new case -- not from the discharge date or the closing date.

Filing bar vs. discharge bar: These waiting periods do not prevent you from filing a new case. They prevent you from receiving a discharge in the new case. You can still file for the automatic stay protection, but without a discharge, creditors can resume collection once the case ends.

The 180-Day Filing Bar -- Section 109(g)

Section 109(g) imposes a 180-day bar on filing a new case if the debtor's prior case was dismissed under certain circumstances:

  • 109(g)(1): The prior case was dismissed for willful failure to abide by court orders or to appear before the court.
  • 109(g)(2): The debtor voluntarily dismissed the prior case after a creditor filed a motion for relief from stay.

Unlike the discharge waiting periods above, Section 109(g) can actually prevent a debtor from filing at all -- not just block the discharge. Courts are split on whether this bar is jurisdictional (meaning the court must dismiss the new case) or waivable (meaning it only applies if a party raises it).

Automatic Stay Limits for Repeat Filers

The automatic stay under Section 362 is the most immediate benefit of filing bankruptcy -- it stops foreclosures, repossessions, wage garnishments, and lawsuits. But for repeat filers, the stay is limited:

Section 362(c)(3) -- One prior dismissal within 1 year: If the debtor had a case pending within the previous year that was dismissed, the automatic stay in the new case expires after 30 days unless the debtor files a motion to extend it. The debtor must show that the new case was filed in good faith.

Section 362(c)(4) -- Two or more dismissals within 1 year: If the debtor had two or more cases pending within the previous year that were dismissed, there is no automatic stay at all in the new case. The debtor must file a motion asking the court to impose the stay, and must show good faith as to all creditors.

How to extend or impose the stay: In both situations, the debtor can file a motion under Section 362(c)(3)(B) or 362(c)(4)(B). The motion should be filed immediately upon filing the new case, ideally on the same day. Courts typically require a hearing within 30 days, and the debtor must demonstrate changed circumstances -- a new job, new income, a viable plan -- that distinguish the new case from the dismissed one.

These stay limitations are one of the most significant consequences of serial filing. Without the automatic stay, a debtor has no protection from creditor action, which defeats much of the purpose of filing.

Why People File Multiple Times

There are legitimate reasons people file more than once. Life happens -- a debtor who completed a Chapter 7 case in 2015 and then faces a catastrophic medical event in 2024 has every right to seek relief again. The Bankruptcy Code contemplates this and allows it.

But a large portion of serial filings are not the result of new financial crises. They are the result of failed prior cases -- cases that were dismissed before the debtor received any benefit. Common causes include:

  • Bare-bones petitions: The attorney files the petition with no schedules, no plan, and no financial documents, triggering dismissal deadlines from day one.
  • Unaffordable Chapter 13 plans: The debtor is placed in a 3-to-5-year payment plan they cannot sustain, leading to missed payments and dismissal.
  • Poor communication: The attorney fails to respond to trustee requests, misses deadlines, or does not keep the debtor informed.
  • Steering to Chapter 13: Some attorneys steer clients into Chapter 13 cases even when Chapter 7 would better serve them, because Chapter 13 generates ongoing fees.

When a case fails due to attorney performance rather than debtor conduct, the debtor often has no choice but to file again -- now with a worse filing history, reduced stay protections, and additional fees to pay. This cycle disproportionately affects low-income debtors who can least afford it.

The intersection of serial filing and attorney quality is an area of growing academic and regulatory interest. Federal Judicial Center data shows that certain districts and certain firms produce dramatically higher rates of repeat filers. These patterns are measurable, and they matter -- because every failed case costs the debtor time, money, credit damage, and often their chance at a fresh start.
